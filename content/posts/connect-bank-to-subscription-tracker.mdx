## How bank integrations and automatic subscription detection work

Understanding the mechanics helps you choose the right method and set expectations.

### 1. Methods of connecting accounts

- **Bank aggregation APIs (third-party connectors):** Companies like Plaid, TrueLayer, and Yodlee act as intermediaries that securely connect apps to thousands of banks. Most subscription apps rely on one or more of these providers.
- **Direct bank APIs:** Large banks sometimes offer direct APIs for apps to integrate. This can be faster and more reliable when available.
- **Manual import (CSV):** You can upload exported transaction histories from your bank or card provider.
- **Screen scraping (legacy):** Some connectors use credential-based scraping where they simulate a login to fetch transactions. This method is declining due to security and reliability concerns.

### 2. How automatic subscription detection works

- **Recurring pattern recognition:** Algorithms scan transaction histories for repeated amounts, merchant names, and intervals (monthly, yearly, weekly). If a transaction repeats consistently, it's flagged as a subscription.
- **Merchant matching:** Known merchants (e.g., Netflix, Spotify) are matched against internal merchant databases to label services quickly.
- **Heuristics and confirmation prompts:** Apps may ask you to confirm whether a detected recurring charge is a subscription.
- **Machine learning improvements:** Over time, ML models learn patterns of new merchants and can handle slightly variable charges by identifying typical ranges and timings.

### 3. Data types pulled

- Transaction date
- Merchant / payee
- Amount
- Transaction description
- Transaction ID
- Account balance (in some cases)

This information helps the tracker decide which charges are subscriptions and estimate renewal dates.

## Step-by-step: How to connect bank to subscription tracker (using usesubwise.app)

Below is a practical flow tailored to usesubwise.app but applicable to most modern subscription trackers.

### Step 1 — Create an account on the subscription tracker

1. Sign up using your email, Google, or Apple account.
2. Complete identity verification steps if required (some apps ask for basic info for security).
3. Configure currency and time zone.

### Step 2 — Choose how you’ll connect accounts

- Prefer the aggregator option (Plaid, TrueLayer) if available — it's usually the easiest and most secure.
- If your bank isn't listed, use the CSV import option or a direct bank API connection.

### Step 3 — Connect a bank account (typical flow)

1. In the app, navigate to the Accounts or Integrations page.
2. Click Add account or Connect bank.
3. Choose your bank from the list.
4. You’ll be redirected to the aggregator’s secure interface.
5. Search and select your bank, then authenticate using your bank credentials or multi-factor authentication (MFA).
6. Select account(s) to share—checking, savings, or credit cards.
7. Grant read-only access. The tracker will not be able to move money.
8. Wait for account syncing to finish — initial sync can take a few minutes to several hours depending on the provider.

**Note:** Most apps will ask for read-only access; they cannot initiate payments unless you explicitly authorize payment features.

### Step 4 — Review detected subscriptions

- Once transactions import, the tracker will present detected recurring charges.
- Confirm which ones are legitimate subscriptions and tag or categorize them.
- Merge duplicates and correct merchant names if necessary.

### Step 5 — Add manual subscriptions

If the app missed a subscription (e.g., billed annually or irregularly), add it manually with name, amount, billing cycle, and next billing date.

### Step 6 — Set alerts and rules

- Configure alerts for upcoming renewals, large changes in recurring amounts, and expiring free trials.
- Create rules to automatically categorize merchants (e.g., label Netflix as Streaming).

## How to link credit card subscriptions specifically

Many subscriptions bill to credit cards rather than bank debit accounts. Linking credit cards ensures you capture services billed on plastic.

### Why credit card data matters

- Credit cards often show merchant descriptors more clearly than bank transfers.
- They capture pre-authorizations and refunds in a way that bank accounts may not.

### Steps to link credit cards

1. On the Integrations page choose Add credit card or Add account.
2. Select the card issuer from the list or choose the aggregator to find it.
3. Authenticate via issuer credentials and MFA.
4. Select the card account(s) to link.
5. Wait for transactions to sync.

### Handling multiple cards

- Link every card you use for recurring services — personal, business, and co-branded cards.
- Label cards in the app (e.g., Personal Amex, Business Chase) for easier reconciliation.

### When link credit card subscriptions fails

- Try manual CSV import from your card issuer.
- Connect the underlying bank if the credit line is part of a bank account.
- Contact the app's support or your card issuer to troubleshoot authentication flow.

## Import transactions into a subscription tracker: CSVs, bank feeds, and APIs

If the direct integration fails or you prefer manual control, importing transactions is a robust fallback.

### CSV import — step-by-step

1. Log into your bank or card account and export transactions (usually in CSV or Excel format).
2. Choose a date range that covers at least 12 months for reliable subscription detection.
3. Ensure columns include date, description, and amount.
4. In usesubwise.app or the tracker, go to Import transactions and upload the CSV.
5. Map the columns to the app fields (date -> date, memo -> merchant, amount -> amount).
6. Confirm the import and wait for processing.
7. Review detected recurring charges and categorize them.

### Automated bank feeds vs CSV

- Automated feeds update continuously without manual exports, keeping subscription detection up to date.
- CSV import is a point-in-time snapshot but useful for historical backfills or when integration is unavailable.

### Import transactions subscription tracker best practices

- Always back up your CSVs locally before uploading.
- Use 12+ months of history when possible — yearly subscriptions might be missed with short ranges.
- Remove unrelated transactions (large one-offs) if they clutter detection.

## Security, privacy, and consent: What to expect

Security is the top concern when you connect financial accounts. Here’s what to look for:

### 1. Read-only access

- Reliable subscription trackers request read-only access. This lets them view transactions but not initiate transfers.

### 2. Aggregator security and encryption

- Aggregators use bank-level encryption and secure tokens for authorization. Your app stores a limited-access token, not raw credentials.

### 3. Two-factor authentication (2FA)

- Most banks require 2FA during the connection process, which adds an extra layer of safety.

### 4. Data retention and deletion

- Review the app’s privacy policy. You should be able to revoke access and delete imported data.

### 5. Regulatory protections

- Aggregators and banks operate under regional regulations (like GDPR in the EU or GLBA in the US) that govern data use and sharing.

### 6. What the app can and cannot do

- Can: Read transaction history, categorize charges, generate reports, send alerts, and recommend cancellations.
- Cannot (unless explicitly authorized): Move money, change bank account settings, or access login passwords stored outside the connector.

## Troubleshooting common integration issues

Even with modern tools, connectors sometimes fail. Here’s a practical checklist to resolve problems.

### Problem: Bank not listed

- Solution: Use CSV import or check for a direct bank API. Contact app support to request adding the bank.

### Problem: Authentication errors or MFA loop

- Solution: Try on a different device or browser; clear cookies; ensure your bank’s mobile passcode or authenticator app is ready. If the bank blocks third-party access, contact your bank.

### Problem: Missing transactions

- Solution: Check the date range of the sync and import older transactions via CSV. Confirm that the linked account is the one used for subscriptions (sometimes a secondary card is billed).

### Problem: Duplicate entries

- Solution: Use the app’s merge or dedupe tools. Ensure you didn’t import the same CSV multiple times.

### Problem: Misclassified merchants

- Solution: Edit merchant names and create a rule to auto-categorize future transactions.

### Problem: Automatic subscription detection missed annual billing

- Solution: Add the subscription manually or upload a 24-month transaction history to help detection algorithms learn the pattern.

## Pro Tips: Make your bank integration work harder

1. **Use multiple connection methods:** Combine an aggregator link for live updates and periodic CSV imports for deep historical analysis.
2. **Keep a 12–24 month history:** This helps the automatic detection identify annual or irregular subscriptions reliably.
3. **Label recurring charges consistently:** Edit merchant names to remove noise (e.g., transform “AMZ.MUSIC” to “Amazon Music”) for clearer reporting.
4. **Set two alerts:** One for trial expirations and one for price increases (e.g., >10% change in recurring charge).
5. **Reconcile quarterly:** Manually review detected subscriptions every three months to catch changes or forgotten services.
6. **Use rules:** Automate categorization to reduce manual work as the system learns.
7. **Monitor credit cards closely:** Most subscription frauds show up on cards first — add cards to your tracker for early detection.
8. **Use a dedicated card for trials:** If you sign up frequently for short-term trials, use one card so the tracker can find and manage those charges more easily.
9. **Export before disconnecting:** If you revoke access, export your subscription list and transaction history first.
10. **Leverage negotiation power:** Use the app’s insights to negotiate lower plans or ask for loyalty discounts.

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

1. **How do I connect bank to subscription tracker securely?**
   - Use a tracker that integrates with a reputable aggregator (Plaid, TrueLayer, Yodlee), grant read-only access, and authenticate via your bank’s MFA. Review the app’s privacy policy and revoke access anytime.

2. **Can I link credit card subscriptions if the card is issued by a different bank?**
   - Yes. Most aggregators and apps support credit cards from different issuers. If an issuer isn’t supported, use CSV import or connect the underlying bank account.

3. **What if the tracker misses an annual subscription?**
   - Upload 12–24 months of transaction history, add the subscription manually, and label the merchant clearly so the algorithm can learn the pattern.

4. **Is automatic subscription detection accurate?**
   - It’s typically high for consistent monthly charges and well-known merchants. Accuracy can drop for variable amounts, irregular billing cycles, or ambiguous merchant descriptors.

5. **Will the app be able to move money or charge my accounts?**
   - Not unless you explicitly give payment authority. Most apps request read-only access by default.

6. **What is the difference between CSV import and live bank integration?**
   - CSV is manual and static (point-in-time). Live integration syncs ongoing transactions automatically and keeps your tracker up to date.

7. **How often will transactions sync?**
   - Sync frequency depends on the aggregator and app — it can be near-real-time, hourly, or daily. Check the app’s documentation for specifics.

8. **Can I manage subscriptions billed on different currencies?**
   - Yes, most trackers support multi-currency transactions, but ensure the app has currency conversion and reporting features you need.

9. **What if my bank blocks third-party access?**
   - Use CSV exports or contact your bank to request permission for third-party aggregators. Some banks provide direct APIs or whitelist apps on request.

10. **How do I cancel a subscription after detection?**
   - The app will usually give details on the billing merchant and contact method. You can cancel via the merchant’s website or sometimes via the app if it offers cancellation support.
